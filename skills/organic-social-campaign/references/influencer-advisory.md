# Influencer Advisory — advice-only

## Scope boundary (read first, and state it in the deliverable)

This skill advises on **whether, what type, and how** to use influencers. It does
**not** source them.

| In scope | Explicitly out of scope |
|---|---|
| Whether influencers fit this campaign at all | Building a named long-list or shortlist |
| Which **archetype** × **engagement mode** fits | Outreach, DMs, or any contact |
| Content **concept directions** and the brief shape | Negotiation, rates, contracting |
| Selection **criteria** the client applies themselves | Vetting specific individuals |
| Disclosure obligations to design for | Legal sign-off on those obligations |
| Measurement design | Running the relationship |

**State this boundary in the deliverable itself**, in one line, so the client
never mistakes an archetype recommendation for a vendor recommendation. If asked
to pick named influencers: **decline and redirect** — produce the archetype +
criteria so the client (or their agency) can select against them. This is an
engagement boundary, not a capability gap: selection carries brand-safety and
contractual risk that belongs with the people who can actually diligence it.

**Selection is client/PM-owned — archetypes travel, names don't.** The
client-facing deliverable ships archetypes + criteria + placeholders; when the
client or a reviewing partner (agency PM) supplies named candidates, theirs
govern the deliverable. If a separately-commissioned research shortlist exists,
it stays an **advisory backup** in the working files — offered, never baked
into the client deck uninvited, and set aside without protest when the
client/PM picks differently.

> **Never-fabricate.** Do not invent an influencer, a follower count, an audience
> overlap figure, or a rate. Archetype bands below are **directional planning
> aids**, not quotes for a named person.

## Archetypes

Thresholds and cost classes are **directional and move constantly** — treat as
planning bands, verify before anyone signs anything.

| Archetype | Followers | Engagement trait | Cost class | B2B fit | B2C fit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nano** | <10k | Highest engagement rate; genuine peer trust; often unpaid/gifted | Very low | **Strong** — niche practitioners are credible to buyers | Moderate — needs volume to matter |
| **Micro** | 10k–100k | Strong engagement with real niche authority; the usual sweet spot | Low–moderate | **Strong** — sector voices, analysts, practitioners | **Strong** — the workhorse tier |
| **Mid** | 100k–500k | Reach grows, engagement rate falls; more transactional | Moderate–high | Weak — audience rarely matches a narrow ICP | **Strong** — awareness and cultural reach |

**The B2B default is nano/micro.** Reach is the wrong objective for a B2B track;
*credibility with a narrow audience* is the objective, and that lives in the
small tiers. Mid-tier is a **B2C awareness** instrument — reasonable for a
consumer track whose job is cultural presence, wrong for a procurement audience.

**Engagement rate falls as follower count rises.** A mid-tier creator's larger
audience is also a *looser* one. If the campaign's job is belief change in a
defined segment, more followers is not better.

## Engagement modes

| Mode | What it is | Best for | Watch out |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gifting / seeding** | Send product/access, no guaranteed post | Cheap discovery; testing fit | No control, no guarantee — still **disclosable** if a relationship exists |
| **Paid post** | Contracted deliverable | Predictable timing (e.g. a peak moment) | Reads as an ad if creative freedom is over-constrained |
| **Content licence / UGC** | Pay to reuse their content on your channels | Getting native-feeling creative for owned/paid surfaces | Usage rights must be explicit up front — scope, surfaces, duration |
| **Ambassador** | Ongoing multi-post relationship | Sustained credibility; repetition builds association | Highest disclosure and brand-safety exposure |

For a campaign with a **peak moment**, paid posts and ambassadors are the
schedulable modes; gifting cannot be timed and should not be load-bearing.

## Selection criteria (the client applies these)

1. **Audience overlap with the ICP** — not follower count. Ask for audience
   demographics/firmographics, not a screenshot of reach.
2. **Engagement authenticity** — comment quality over volume; sudden follower
   step-changes; comments that don't discuss the actual content; engagement
   pods.
3. **Brand-safety screen** — past content, adjacent affiliations, category
   conflicts. Look back further than the last month.
4. **Values fit** — for an advocacy campaign especially: does their existing
   position contradict the campaign's argument? A creator who has argued the
   opposite is a liability, however well they perform.
5. **Prior/competing work** — are they already running an adjacent campaign on
   the same evidence? That may be continuity (good) or conflict (see
   [campaign-narrative-arc.md](campaign-narrative-arc.md) § Differentiation vs
   prior waves).

## Concept brief template

The brief the client hands a selected influencer. **Recommend the shape; do not
fill it with invented specifics.**

| Section | Contents |
|---|---|
| **Campaign context** | the belief shift, in one line — what the audience thinks now vs after |
| **Beat alignment** | which act/beat this lands in, and the date window |
| **Mandatory message** | the one claim that must survive; sourced |
| **Creative freedom envelope** | what they may change (format, tone, angle, their own story) vs what they may not (the claim, the data, the disclosure) |
| **Deliverable spec** | surface, count, length, aspect, captions |
| **Disclosure requirement** | explicit — see below; never left to the creator to figure out |
| **Usage rights ask** | may the client reuse it? where, how long, organic or paid? |

**Over-constrained creative is the most common failure.** The reason to use a
creator is that their audience trusts *their* voice; a script removes the only
thing you were buying. Constrain the **claim**, free the **telling**.

## Disclosure baseline (NL + EU)

> **Advisory, not legal counsel.** This is what to *design for*. Regulated
> sectors (health, finance, food claims) escalate to **tier-3 QA** (legal + SME +
> brand) — and any live campaign gets actual legal review.

**Netherlands — Reclamecode Social Media & Influencer Marketing (RSM).** The
Dutch self-regulatory code requires that a **relevant relationship** between
advertiser and creator — paid, gifted, or otherwise material — is **clearly
recognisable** to the audience, and it places a **duty of care on the
advertiser**: the brand is expected to inform the creator of the rules, not
outsource compliance to them. Current text:
https://www.reclamecode.nl/nederlandse-reclame-code/bijzondere-reclamecodes/
(accessed 2026-07-14).

**EU — consumer law.** Commercial communications must be **transparently
disclosed**; undisclosed paid content is treated as a misleading practice. The
Commission's Influencer Legal Hub is the practitioner entry point:
https://commission.europa.eu/topics/consumers/consumer-rights-and-complaints/influencer-legal-hub_en
(accessed 2026-07-14).

**Designing for it:**

- Disclosure is **prominent and up-front** — not buried mid-caption, not hashtag
  soup at the end.
- `#reclame` / `#ad` and platform "paid partnership" labels are **examples of
  practice, not a safe harbour**. A label does not cure a post whose commercial
  nature is otherwise disguised, and the platform toggle alone may not satisfy
  the code.
- **Gifting counts.** "They weren't paid" is not an exemption — a material
  relationship is disclosable.
- The **advertiser** carries the duty of care: brief the rules explicitly and
  keep a record that you did.

## Measurement

Match the metric to the mode's job:

- **Audience-overlap delivered** vs promised (the only number that matters for
  B2B fit).
- **Engagement quality** — comments that engage the *argument*, not "🔥".
- **Sends/shares** — on Instagram the top-tier ranking signal, now outweighing
  saves (Hootsuite, *Instagram algorithm*, accessed 2026-07-14 —
  https://blog.hootsuite.com/instagram-algorithm/ ; see also
  [platforms/meta.md](platforms/meta.md)). A creator post that gets forwarded is
  working — and a forward is a peer recommendation, which is the entire reason
  to use a creator rather than buy an impression.
- **Assisted/self-reported attribution** — "saw it via X" on intake forms.
  Influencer impact is dark-funnel by nature (see
  [measurement-benchmarks.md](measurement-benchmarks.md)); last-click will
  under-read it.
- **Earned follow-on** — did anyone in the ICP respond, quote, or continue it?

## Red flags

- Follower step-changes, or engagement that doesn't match audience size.
- Generic comments ("Great post!") — pod behaviour.
- Reluctance to disclose, or a request to "keep it subtle".
- Refusal to share audience data.
- A back catalogue contradicting the campaign's argument.
- Rate quoted purely on follower count, with no audience evidence.
- Already running an unrelated campaign in the same category this week.
