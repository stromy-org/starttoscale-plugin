# Executive & Founder-Led Content — B2B Reference

The primary organic distribution engine in 2026. On LinkedIn especially, personal
profiles dominate the feed while company pages are throttled (see
`strategy-foundations.md`, Shift 1). Executive- and founder-led content is *not*
a subset of employee advocacy — it is a distinct, higher-touch program with its
own sourcing, ghostwriting, cadence, and measurement. Run it as the spine of the
strategy; the brand Page is the credibility anchor around it.

> **Real people only — never fabricate an executive.** Every named executive,
> founder, quote, POV, or bio in this program must trace to a real person in
> `company_context.people[]`. If that list is empty or absent, do **not** invent a
> spokesperson, attribute a POV to a named individual, or ghostwrite "as the CEO"
> for a person you cannot verify. Ask the user who to feature, or keep content at
> the org/brand level with no named individual. (This is the within-overlay twin
> of the no-overlay STOP in the main skill.)

## Executive-led vs employee advocacy — two programs, one system

| | Executive / founder-led | Employee advocacy |
|---|---|---|
| **Who** | 1–5 named leaders (CEO, founder, practice leads) | 8–15+ employees across seniority |
| **Goal** | Category authority, narrative ownership, high-intent inbound | Reach breadth, culture, talent, amplification |
| **Content** | Mostly original POV, often ghostwritten with heavy input | Mix of original + company content with personal angle |
| **Effort/touch** | High — dedicated ghostwriting + review per person | Moderate — content queue + light coaching |
| **Cadence** | 2–4 high-quality posts/week per leader | 1–3 posts/week per advocate |
| **Measurement** | Person-level authority, inbound, pipeline influence | Program-level reach, active-advocate %, incremental reach |

They reinforce each other: executives set the narrative; advocates extend it.
Design both, but if capacity is scarce, **fund the executive program first** — it
has the highest reported ROI (lower cost per qualified engagement, higher
conversion) and C-suite visibility lifts advocate participation.

## Profile-as-channel

An executive's profile is a landing page, not a résumé. Optimize before posting:

- **Headline** — what they help clients achieve + point of view, not just title.
  "Helping infrastructure firms win government trust" > "Managing Director at X".
- **About** — first-person narrative: the problem they obsess over, proof, and
  who they help. Front-load the first 2–3 lines (that's what shows before "see
  more").
- **Featured** — pin 2–3 best-performing or most-representative posts/artifacts.
- **Banner + profile photo** — professional, human, on-brand; consistent with the
  company's visual identity from `brand_context.json`.
- **Activity** — the profile is judged by recent posts *and* by the quality of
  their comments on others' posts. Commenting is part of the channel.

## The content engine (sourcing → draft → review → publish)

The hardest part of exec-led content is **sourcing genuine insight** without
consuming the executive's time. The engine:

1. **Capture, don't compose.** Get raw material from the leader in the lowest-
   friction way: a 20–30 min monthly voice/interview session, voice notes, a
   recurring 3-question prompt, or mining their existing talks, client calls
   (with consent), internal memos, and Q&A. The insight must be *theirs*; the
   words can be drafted.
2. **Draft in their voice.** Ghostwrite from the captured material, running the
   copy through the org **voice cascade** (L1 baseline + L2 client voice) *and*
   the executive's individual voice (how they actually talk — cadence, favorite
   framings, what they'd never say). Ghostwriting that sounds generic is the #1
   failure mode.
3. **Executive review = approve/edit, not blank-page.** The leader reacts to a
   near-final draft (fast) rather than writing from scratch (slow). Keep edits in
   a shared doc; log recurring corrections to sharpen the voice model.
4. **Publish from their own account**, in their own hand where possible. Schedule
   for their audience's peak windows; the leader (or a delegate) engages with
   early comments in the first hour — the algorithm rewards author replies.

## Executive content archetypes

Bias toward original POV; these consistently earn authority for B2B leaders:

- **The staked POV** — a clear, defensible opinion on where the field is going.
- **The contrarian correction** — challenge a widely-held assumption, with
  evidence. (High-risk/high-reward; must be genuinely defensible.)
- **The decision story** — "here's a hard call we made and what we learned"
  (anonymized where needed). Narrative + stakes + lesson.
- **The pattern from the field** — "across N engagements, I keep seeing X."
  Authority from repeated observation.
- **The teardown / framework** — a named model from their practice. Highly saved.
- **The human-professional** — a personal reflection that carries a professional
  lesson. Builds the "face" audiences follow.
- **The generous amplification** — spotlight a client/peer/idea (not self). Builds
  reciprocity and network reach.

## AEO/GEO fit

Executive posts are prime **citation** material for AI answer engines when they
carry named-expert quotes and statistics (see `aeo-geo-social.md`). Where
appropriate, structure a leader's POV so a single passage stands alone as a
quotable, attributable claim — that is what gets pulled into an AI answer.

## Governance

- **Guardrails, not scripts.** Executives get more latitude than advocates, but
  the same hard lines apply: no confidential client info, no non-public
  financials, no commitments on behalf of the company, no unvetted legal/
  regulatory/crisis statements. Regulated sectors: compliance review before
  publishing.
- **Ghostwriting ethics.** The insight and endorsement must be genuinely the
  executive's; they approve every post. Never publish a view the named person has
  not actually signed off on.
- **Attribution honesty.** If the leader has no real POV on a topic, don't
  manufacture one — pick a different topic. (Ties back to the empty-people guard.)

## Measurement (person-level)

Track per executive, not just program-wide:

- **Authority** — follower growth of the *individual*, profile views, search
  appearances, inbound connection quality (are the right people following?).
- **Engagement quality** — substantive comments and saves > raw reactions; a
  comment-quality rubric (see `measurement-benchmarks.md`).
- **Narrative** — is the leader becoming associated with the target topic? (share
  of voice on the theme; AI-citation appearances).
- **Pipeline influence** — self-reported attribution ("saw our CEO's posts"),
  inbound referencing their content, meetings sourced. Dark-funnel aware
  (see `measurement-benchmarks.md`).

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Symptom | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic ghostwriting** | Posts sound like any exec anywhere | Capture real material; model the individual's voice; approve/edit not compose |
| **Executive burnout / dropout** | Strong start, silent after 3 weeks | Minimize their ask (react to drafts); monthly capture session; ruthless reuse |
| **Inauthentic POV** | Takes the leader wouldn't actually defend | Source from their real views; drop topics they don't own |
| **Inconsistency** | Sporadic posting kills algorithmic trust | Buffer 2–3 weeks ahead; delegate scheduling; protect cadence |
| **Approval bottleneck** | Drafts die waiting for sign-off | Fast review ritual (shared doc, 48h SLA); pre-agreed safe topics need no extra review |
| **All self, no generosity** | Feels like a broadcast, low reach | Mix in amplification and genuine engagement on others' posts |

## Sustainability

- **Reuse relentlessly** — one capture session → multiple posts, comments,
  newsletter fodder, and long-form video talking points (COPE, see the main
  skill's Phase 7).
- **Protect the cadence** — a delegate handles scheduling and first-hour comment
  triage so the leader only supplies insight and approval.
- **Close the loop** — show the executive their authority metrics monthly; nothing
  sustains a leader's participation like seeing the right people follow and inbound
  referencing their content.
